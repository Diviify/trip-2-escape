Himachali cuisine is built for cold nights and long winters — hearty, warming, and far more varied than the standard North Indian restaurant menu suggests.

## Dham: the festive feast

**Dham** is a ceremonial meal served on brass plates at weddings and festivals. Expect rice, madra (yogurt-based chickpeas), rajma, and sweet rice. In Kullu and Mandi, community dham events welcome respectful visitors during festival season.

## Siddu: the steamed bread

**Siddu** — stuffed steamed bread filled with poppy seeds or walnuts — is Manali's signature snack. Find it at Old Manali cafés and village homestays. Dip it in ghee for the authentic experience.

## Tibetan influences in Lahaul-Spiti

Momos, thukpa, and butter tea reflect Buddhist Himalayan culture. Key Monastery villages serve some of the freshest momos you'll taste anywhere in India.

### Local ingredients

Wild apricots, buckwheat, and mountain rajma define the pantry. Ask hosts about **chha gosht** (marinated lamb) if you're invited to a family meal — it's a point of pride.

## Where to eat

Heritage hotels in Shimla serve refined versions; village homestays offer authenticity. In Spiti, meals are often included with lodging — embrace whatever the family is cooking that day.

## Dietary notes

Vegetarian travellers fare well with dham and siddu. Vegan options are limited in remote areas — communicate needs clearly and carry snacks for high-altitude trekking days.
